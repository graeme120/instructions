A Scoried Past

An exploration of the historical evolution of western music notation

Introduction

Clay tablet inscribed with the Hurrian Hymn, the world’s oldest surviving melody, dating back at least 3,400 years.

The world supposedly began with a sound. A bang! In contemporary culture, it is not a figure or image we associate with the origins of life, but merely a sound.

Sound is a tool far older than mankind, one that has been with us since our inception. It will forever be a mystery why and how music emerged from sound, but its origins are undoubtedly linked to the evolution of human-made tools and spoken language.

Music notation is wildly predated by music itself, and just like language, was passed on purely through oral tradition. Moreover, the emergence of musical notation denotes the threshold between the prehistory and history of music.

In Ancient Greece, it was Pythagoras who first discovered a proportional relationship between music notes. Two notes which make an interval of an octave always have a ratio of 2:1. A perfect fifth is made with the ratio of 3:2, and a perfect 4th with a ratio of 4:3. Combinations of these intervals then create the other notes which make up the major scale.

Pythagorean music theory as represented by the circle of fifths.

The ancient Greeks forever changed music by imposing a rational order onto it. Suddenly sound could be ‘notes’, if produced a certain way. Music, an outpouring of human emotion, had found a container, a rational form to give it structure. Nevertheless, it would be a few centuries more before this new language became a visual one.

Evolution of the Tool

The earliest version of today’s standard musical notation came from the European monasteries, and was used as a mnemonic device for the recitation of Gregorian chants. Their system used neumes, inflection marks that indicate the relative pitch compared to other neumes. As it did not indicate rhythm or exact pitch, these marks were designed for monks already familiar with the chant, and did not lend itself to the creation of new music.

Neumian song

Somewhere at the turn of 1000 AD, Guido D’Arezzo, a Benedictine monk in Italy, popularized the use of staff notation, using a red line to indicate the position of the note f and a yellow line for the note c, working in a similar way to our modern base and treble clefs. He taught the use of solmization syllables based on a hymn to Saint John the Baptist:

Ut was later changed to the clearer syllable ‘Do’, as in dominus.

Guido’s notational system had some important differences from the one in use today. Most notably, his system didn’t communicate the duration for which a note should be held, which massively influenced Fthe of the time; giving it a legato (smooth and connected) sound, since each note’s duration was up to the singer to interpret.

Guido song

Religious institutions would continue to act as the cultural outlet for music production for the next few hundred years.,he church was the largest producer of notated music. Spatially, its existence was limited to the walls of the church, where the acoustics provided by its high ceilings further amplified the music’s spiritual power.

The following centuries saw gradual modifications and expansions of the system used by the church. In the 14th century, Philippe de Vitry developed mensural notation, which communicated both pitch and rhythm using notes of different shapes.

Manuscript of Vitry's Aman novi/Heu Fortuna/Heu me, tristis est anima mea

Mensural notation is its modern name, derived from the terminology of medieval theorists who described it as musica mensura (measured music), as opposed to musica plana, referring to Gregorian Chant. Up until this point, notated music and oral tradition had coexisted, as oral tradition supplemented information that notation could not contain. Now that pitch and rhythm could be communicated visually, the use of musical notation rose in prominence, and over time musica plana remained only within the church

mensural song

The renaissance period saw a number of these improvements and revisions to the notational standard. As compositions become more complex in terms of key and rhythm, the four-time-signature system introduced by de Vitry is abandoned in favor of our modern ‘fractional-style’ system.

Widespread Adoption & Refinement

The Concert, Gerard van Honthorst, 1623

The Renaissance period, thanks to crucial technological advancements like the invention of the printing press, saw notated music spread across Europe. As instruments continued to expand in terms of sound making capability, more non-vocal music rose in popularity. Notations flexibility across all instruments became a uniting force, allowing orchestras to form and play under a shared language. Through mere marks and lines, sound could now organize itself into mankind’s most powerful expressions of pure feeling.

Now divorced from the church, typically in service of wealthy aristocrats, and with an orchestra of sounds at their disposal, composers became celebrity figures and were celebrated for their artistic genius.

It is here where we see the true emergence of musical style, wherein composers became known for their specific sound and compositional technique. By this point, notation as a tool was refined, and offered composers both the structure and freedom to translate human emotion into breathtaking works of music.

bach song
"I play the notes as they are written, but it is God who makes the music.”
Johann Sebastian Bach

More than a tool, notation is a language of its own, allowing it to transcend speech and translation. Compared to the physicality of painting or sculpture, notation as a set of instructions enabled it to move farther and faster, inviting all of Europe to participate and contribute to a shared cultural experience. Composers, masters of this international language, learn from one another and in turn push orchestral music to new stylistic heights.

The transition from Baroque to classical music in the 18th century inflected a certain energy into composition that notation struggled to keep up with, leading to the introduction of staccatos, slurs and a more expressive use of dynamics. As composers pushed against the limits of their tool, the tool itself was evolved to service their artistic demands.

Classical music eventually gives way to the Romantic period, compositional music grows ever more popular, and the limitations of musical notation are pushed ever further. As time goes on, musical notation begins to standardize; the number of clefs used is reduced to the three still used in modern scores- G (treble), F (bass) and C (alto/tenor).

Experimental Futures

Succession, Wassily Kandinsky, 1935

The 20th century, as was the case with almost all artistic tradition, saw a radical break with tradition and the artistic continuum that defined previous centuries. This by no means was the end of notation, in fact notation had reached such ubiquity that massive modifications to the tool’s standard form would be enormously confusing and expensive. Textbooks, curriculums, thousands of pages of sheet music would need revision, not to mention the millions of musicians who would need to re-learn sight reading. No, standard notation could no longer evolve in the way it had in the past. Nevertheless, the modern era would find new ways to continue pushing the limits of notation.

What the 20th century brought were demonstrations of alternative forms of musical notation. These iterations of the tool no longer strived to represent the ideas of the composer with accuracy, but instead subvert viewer’s expectations of a musical performance.

4'33" (In Proportional Notation) John Cage, 1952/53

John Cage was an American composer and music theorist, born in 1912. One of his most seminal works was 4’33, a composition that featured four minutes and thirty-three seconds of silence. The accompanying notation is equally defiant, consisting of only the title and playing instructions to legitimize the blank page’s claim to be a composition.

Toshi Ichiyanagi, a Japanese composer and student of Cage’s, would further the work of his mentor and contribute heavily to the emerging graphic notation movement of the 1950s. Graphic notation is defined by its variability and lack of standardization, used to indicate both extremely precise and intentionally imprecise musical instruction. It often incorporates elements of modern art into its visual appearance, and as such is often displayed as a visual piece removed from the music it indicates.

Music For Electric Metronome, Toshi Ichiyanagi
IBM for Merce Cunningham Toshi Ichiyanagi, 1960

IBM for Merce Cunningham, took visual cues from IBM’s computer cards, physical pieces of paper used to scan information directly into computers which, in 1960, took up an entire building floor. Ichiyanagi’s IBM composition would unknowingly foretell the future of music notation, correctly predicting the power of computers in revolutionizing the way music is stored, recorded and communicated.

Abstracted Form, Technological Function

Impossible music #9 Albert Bernal, 2006

The 1990s saw the rise of audio-composition software, which found new ways to represent music notes while adapting to the technology of the time. MIDI (short for Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a communications protocol that allows electronic instruments to communicate data about musical instructions digitally. Decades prior, early electronic instruments popularized the MIDI format, and as audio-editing software rose in popularity, a new visual form was born to represent this futuristic notational form.

MIDI notes performing a song on a virtual piano
Example of a workspace in the popular audio-editing software, Ableton

The visual representation of MIDI files varies between software, but tends to take the shape of a simple rectangle, only communicating the relative duration of the audio contained by the MIDI file. Despite their appearance, these rectangles contain an incredible amount of musical data, which is then accessed and modified using other parts of the software.

MIDI files, and the popularization of visually-abstracted, data-driven audio, signals an end to one of standard notation's most foundational purposes. While notation is still used for physical performances, physical instruments are no longer necessary for the production of music- the data contained within MIDI files can be endlessly replicated, modified and reconstructed.

MIDI files offer possibilities for music production that couldn’t have been fathomed even decades prior, which has naturally inspired composers to push the tool to its absolute limits. Black MIDI is a musical subgenre that involves packing as many MIDI notes into a single composition as possible, resulting in mesmerizing visuals only half as chaotic as the music they produce. Black refers to the hypothetical overlapping of music notes on a page, so crowded together they turn the page black.

Visual of the Black Midi song TSMB2, The Destroyer!

Conclusions

The history of music notation is an overlap of many separate histories; the history of music, technology, religion and, most notably, humanity itself. Notation establishes a structure to visually communicate the music being played, which in turn influences the creation of new music, and new ways of innovating beyond the limits of the existing structure. Ration and emotion, these two opposing ideals find common ground in the notation and performance of music, and push each other forward in an endless discovery of new possibilities for human expression.
